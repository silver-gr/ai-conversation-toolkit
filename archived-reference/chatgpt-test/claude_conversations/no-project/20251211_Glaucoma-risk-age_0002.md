## Context for Continuation

**Last User Query:**
> At what age is one at risk for glaucoma? 

**Last Assistant Response (excerpt):**
> Glaucoma is one of those sneaky eye villains that doesn’t care about your schedule, but it *does* care about your age curve. Risk isn’t a cliff you fall off — it’s a ramp that gets steeper as the decades stack up.

Here’s the clean, no-nonsense breakdown:

**• Under 40:**  
Risk is low unless you have specific factors (strong family history, high eye pressure, past eye injuries, steroid use, severe myopia, or certain systemic conditions). Think “possible, but unlikely.”

**• Ages 40–60:**  
This is where the real climb begins. The risk starts increasing meaningfully after 40, and eye doctors usually recommend screening every 2–4 years.

**• Over 60:**  
Risk rises sharply. This is prime glaucoma territory. Most new diagnoses happen here. Screening every 1–2 years is generally advised.

**• Over 70:**  
Risk accelerates even more — age itself becomes one of the strongest predictors.

A slightly brutal truth: glaucoma is often symptomless until damage is done, which is why it gets nicknamed “the silent thief of sight.” Early detection is the whole game.

If you want, I can map your personal risk profile based on family history, vision issues, medications, and habits — but even without that, regular checks after 40 are the smart play.
